The why

The dream came first; the data gave me a way to chase it.

Growing up in Kwale, I watched girls quietly disappear from school for reasons no one was counting. One was as ordinary as a missing pack of sanitary towels: without them, girls missed days, then weeks, then stopped coming back at all. I wanted to fix that long before I knew what a regression was.

Years later, the data finally gave me a way. In a community enrollment campaign back home, the numbers made the invisible visible: girls starting grade one at thirteen and fourteen, attendance dropping around those same missing essentials. Putting hard evidence behind it helped turn quiet concern into action, and helped bring monthly sanitary-towel distribution into those schools.

Who is being missed, and what would it take to reach them?

When the dream became real

At Kenya's Institute of Curriculum Development, that question became my full-time job: national student data, special-needs fieldwork across the country, analysis that shaped real curriculum and inclusion decisions. For the first time, my work was changing outcomes for thousands of students I would never meet. That is still the whole point of all of it.
